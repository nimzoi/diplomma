## **Lecture: Conducting a Literature Review for an Engineering Thesis**

### **1. Introduction**

Today’s lecture focuses on a crucial element of your engineering thesis — **the literature review**.
A literature review is not simply a list of articles or books that you have read. It is a **structured, critical analysis** of existing research, technologies, and theories relevant to your topic. Its main goal is to show that you understand the current state of knowledge in your field and that you can position your own work within that context.

---

### **2. Purpose of a Literature Review**

A well-prepared literature review serves several purposes:

1. **Contextualization** – It places your research problem within the broader academic and technical background.
2. **Identification of gaps** – It shows what has already been studied and what remains unexplored.
3. **Justification of your research** – It explains why your work is necessary and how it contributes to solving a real engineering problem.
4. **Foundation for methodology** – It helps you select appropriate methods, tools, and technologies based on prior work.

---

### **3. Structure of a Literature Review**

Although the structure may vary slightly depending on your university’s guidelines, it usually includes:

* **Introduction:** Define the scope of the review — what topics, years, or technologies are covered.
* **Thematic or chronological discussion:**

  * *Thematic* – organized around key concepts or technologies.
  * *Chronological* – showing how the research evolved over time.
* **Critical evaluation:** Don’t just summarize. Compare, contrast, and assess the strengths and weaknesses of different sources.
* **Summary:** Conclude by identifying trends, research gaps, and how your project fits into this context.

---

### **4. Evaluating Sources**

When collecting literature, pay attention to:

* **Credibility:** Use peer-reviewed journals, academic books, and reputable conference papers.
* **Relevance:** The material must relate directly to your research problem.
* **Recency:** Especially in engineering, where technology evolves rapidly, focus on recent publications (typically within the last 5–10 years).
* **Objectivity:** Avoid biased or commercial sources unless you critically discuss them.

---

### **5. Citing and Referencing**

Academic integrity is vital. Every time you use someone else’s idea, data, figure, or wording, you must **cite** the source.

**Note:** For final submission, your institution's formal requirements apply. If Task 09 specifies a particular citation format (e.g. footnotes, *Ibidem*, *op. cit.*), follow Task 09 for the submitted thesis.

There are two main styles commonly used in engineering:

* **IEEE style:**
  Citations are numbered in square brackets, e.g., *“According to [3]...”*
  References are listed numerically at the end.
* **APA or Harvard style:**
  Uses author–date format, e.g., *(Smith, 2021)*, with an alphabetical reference list at the end.

#### **Examples:**

* *IEEE style in-text citation:*
  “Control algorithms for robotic systems are discussed in [5].”
* *APA style in-text citation:*
  “Control algorithms for robotic systems are discussed by Smith (2021).”

---

### **6. Paraphrasing vs. Quoting**

When using others’ work:

* **Paraphrasing:** Express the idea in your own words and cite the source. This shows understanding and is preferred in engineering writing.
* **Direct quoting:** Use quotation marks and cite the page number if you must use the exact wording. Use quotes sparingly — only when the exact phrasing is crucial.

**Example of paraphrasing:**
Original: “Nanocomposites improve the mechanical properties of polymers significantly.”
Your version: “The integration of nanocomposites enhances the strength and durability of polymer materials (Kumar, 2020).”

---

### **7. Using Sources to Inspire Your Own Writing**

You may **build on** other research by:

* Extending an existing model or algorithm.
* Applying a known method to a new context.
* Combining insights from multiple studies to design your own approach.

Always make clear what part of your work is **based on existing knowledge** and what part is **your original contribution**.

---

### **8. Creating Footnotes and Bibliography**

#### **Footnotes:**

Footnotes are short references or explanations placed at the bottom of the page.
In technical and engineering theses, footnotes are less common than end-of-text references but may be used to:

* Clarify terms or abbreviations.
* Provide secondary sources or background information.

Example of a footnote (APA style):

> The method was originally proposed in 1987.^1
>
> ^1 See: T. Brown, *Fundamentals of Signal Processing*, Springer, 1987.

#### **Bibliography or Reference List:**

At the end of your thesis, list all sources you cited.

* Follow a consistent citation style (IEEE, APA, Harvard, etc.).
* Include all necessary information: author(s), title, publication, year, publisher, and DOI or URL (if available).

**Example – IEEE style:**

```
[1] J. K. Author, “Title of paper,” *Journal Name*, vol. 45, no. 3, pp. 123–130, 2021.
[2] A. Smith, *Mechanical Systems Design*, Oxford University Press, 2019.
```

**Example – APA style:**

```
Author, J. K. (2021). Title of paper. *Journal Name*, 45(3), 123–130.
Smith, A. (2019). *Mechanical Systems Design*. Oxford University Press.
```

---

### **9. Common Mistakes to Avoid**

* Relying too heavily on websites or non-scientific sources.
* Copying text directly without citation (plagiarism).
* Including irrelevant or outdated studies.
* Listing sources without critical analysis.
* Mixing citation styles.

---

### **10. Conclusion**

The literature review is not just an academic requirement; it is the intellectual backbone of your engineering thesis.
It demonstrates your ability to think critically, understand your field, and identify where your work contributes to solving real-world problems.
Remember — write with integrity, cite correctly, and let existing research inspire, not replace, your own ideas.
