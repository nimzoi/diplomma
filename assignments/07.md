# Task 07: Results, Discussion and Presentation

**Grading:** up to 10 points.

---

## Purpose of the Results and Discussion Chapter

This chapter presents the **outcomes of your work** in a clear, structured way and **interprets** them in the context of your aims and the literature. It links the methodology (Tasks 04–06) to the conclusions (Task 08).

---

## 1. Presenting Results

### 1.1 Structure

- **Organise by experiment or research question**, not by tool or dataset.
- For each experiment or model:
  * State what you did (briefly).
  * Show results (tables, figures).
  * Interpret and discuss (what it means, why it might be so).

### 1.2 Tables and Figures

- **Number and caption** every table and figure (e.g. *Table 5.1*, *Figure 5.2*).
- **Refer in the text:** e.g. “Table 5.1 shows that…”, “As illustrated in Figure 5.2…”.
- **Placement:** close to the first reference; avoid orphaned captions.
- **Titles:** above tables; below figures (or follow your institution’s template).
- **Source:** indicate source below (own work, dataset, or citation).

### 1.3 What to Include

- **Performance metrics:** e.g. accuracy, F1, RMSE, with confidence intervals or standard deviation where relevant.
- **Comparisons:** baseline vs your model(s); ablation or sensitivity if applicable.
- **Visualisations:** e.g. learning curves, confusion matrices, ROC/PR curves, error analysis (examples of correct/incorrect predictions).
- **Summary statistics:** for data or experiments (sample sizes, splits, key parameters).

---

## 2. Discussion

### 2.1 Interpretation

- **Explain** what the results mean in engineering/scientific terms.
- **Relate to aims:** did you meet the objectives? To what extent?
- **Compare with literature:** do your results align or differ from prior work? Why might that be?

### 2.2 Limitations

- **Be explicit** about limitations:
  * Data (size, quality, bias, domain).
  * Methods (assumptions, simplifications).
  * Evaluation (metrics, single split vs cross-validation, external validity).
- **Avoid** sounding defensive; present limitations as part of a balanced assessment.

### 2.3 Strengths and Weaknesses of the Solution

- Summarise the main **strengths** of your approach and results.
- Acknowledge **weaknesses** and, if possible, suggest how they could be addressed (e.g. in future work).

---

## 3. Academic Style

- Use **third person** or **passive voice**; avoid first person in formal sections.
- Use **past tense** for what you did and found; **present tense** for general statements and figures/tables (“Figure 5.1 shows…”).
- Keep language **precise and neutral**; avoid overclaiming.
